http://www.concordia.ca/content/concordia/en/finearts/art-history/student-life/graduate-profiles/ma/saenz.htmlSaint Joseph and Saint James the Indian-Slayer: An Analysis of the Visual Rhetoric of Sanctity, Masculinity, and Christianization in the Transatlantic Hispanic World, 1500-1700 (tentative)MA - Art HistoryDr. Steven Stowell  BiographyDaniel Santiago Sáenz Tabares is a historian of Hispanic visual and religious cultures, whose research examines the intersections of sanctity and masculinity in the Early Modern transatlantic world, Counter-Reformation spirituality, art, and art theory in Europe and the so-called New World, as well as the pedagogical role of images in the Christianization of the Americas. He holds a B.A. with Great Distinction, Honours in Religion, minor in Art History from Concordia University (2016), where he is currently pursuing a Master's degree in Art History.Under the mentorship of Dr. Steven Stowell, Daniel’s research project expands on his Honours Thesis, titled “What Kind of Husband Are You? The Role of Saint Joseph in the Spiritual and Sexual Conquest of New Spain.” His MA thesis will examine the role of two male saints, namely Joseph and Santiago the “Indian-Slayer,” in the colonization and Christianization of New Spain. This research is supported by the Joseph-Armand Bombardier Canada Graduate Scholarship—Master’s (awarded by the Social Sciences and Humanities Research Council, 2016-2017), the Bourse de maîtrise en recherche (awarded by the Fonds de recherche du Québec - société et culture, 2017-2018), and the Faculty of Fine Arts Fellowship (2016-2017 / 2017-2018).Some of Daniel’s writing has been published in the Concordia Undergraduate Journal of Art History (CUJAH), the CRSA Academic Journal of Religion, as well as a number of exhibition catalogues. In 2016, he curated a show for the Art Matters Festival titled “The Body: Religious and Sacrilegious” (www.bodiesmatter.weebly.com).
